Born in the USA - and living somewhere else in the USA

Internal migration patterns in America

Rosie Thomas

21 December 2024

Where Do People Move?

The US is known for attracting high levels of immigration, and people in search of the "American Dream". Out of a population of 340 million, 51 million people currently living in the country were born somewhere else in the world.

However, the country also experiences high levels of internal migration, as people move for education, job opportunities, and new lives in other parts of the country.

Almost 1/3 of all people born in the United States live somewhere other than their birth state.

So, which state are you most likely to leave? And where do they end up?

Moving from the centre

From the above graph, using data from the US Census Bureau, we see that the most common states to move away from are Wyoming and North Dakota; in both states, more than half of the people who were born there are now living somewhere else.

To find these numbers, I found the "percentage loss" of each state, by finding the percentage difference between the number of people born in that state and the current population of residents in that state who were born there.

Heading to the edge

Where do people end up, if they do move? The below chart shows the states that people who move are most likely to move to.

If all inter-state moves were equally attractive, then each of the 50 states would recieve exactly 2% of moves. We can see that there is actually a wide variation in the distribution of moves from one state to another across the US.

Texas has one of the lowest rates of people moving away after being born there, at 18%. Below, we see that it also receives a relatively high proportion of all internal movers, with 7.56% of movers choosing the state.

Well-known as a "snowbird" retirement location, Florida recieves the highest amount of internal migration; more than 10% of people who live outside their birth state now live in Florida.

We also see that California receives the third-highest percentage of total moves, with almost 6 million people chosing the state as their residence.

Overall Patterns

How do these patterns balance out overall?

Some states seem to have high proportions of people leaving, and also receive a smaller percentage of internal migrants. However, for states with small populations to start with, these numbers might actually not mean that the population is decreasing.

A state that seems popular as a destination might actually be losing its native residents at the same time. So, I calculated an "attraction index" for each state, as follows:

Attraction Index = (population gain as a % of the population) / (population decrease as a % of all births in the state)

An index of 1, or close to 1, is "stasis", meaning that the loss and gain in population are roughly similar. The state is attracting enough people to compensate for the loss of in-state residents.

An index above 1 (states in green) indicates the degree to which the population of the state has been increased by receiving inter-state movers.

A lower index (states in pink) means that the population has been reduced by leaving after being born there. The closer to 0 the index, the higher the degree of this reduction.

A more complicated picture

We see that, despite people born in Wyoming being most likely to leave, the number of people moving there actually seems to balance that out. It has an attraction index slightly above 1.

On the flip side, California - despite receiving the third-highest number of inter-state moves - actually loses more people who were born there.

And, interestingly, despite the perception that some might have of New York being the place that people move to find themselves, or their dreams - it actually has one of the lower attraction indices. 41% of people born there move away, while only 18% of it's US-born population is from another state.
